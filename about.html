About the Archive

The Silvered Glass Archive was founded in 2015 by photographic conservator Eleanor Ashworth, with the mission of recovering and contextualising the photographic heritage of the Victorian and Edwardian eras.

Eleanor Ashworth

Photographic Conservator & Historian · Edinburgh, Scotland

I came to photographic restoration by an indirect route. As a student of art history in Edinburgh, I spent much of my time in the archives of the Scottish National Portrait Gallery, where I first encountered the extraordinary richness of Victorian photography — and the heartbreaking fragility of those early images.

A chance afternoon with a collection of deteriorating albumen prints belonging to a retired schoolteacher in Perthshire convinced me that there was serious and important work to be done. The photographs were beautiful. They were also disintegrating. Nobody in the family knew who the subjects were. Nobody had looked at them in perhaps fifty years.

"These people sat in front of a camera at some point in their lives and were still. They wanted to be seen. We owe it to them to make that possible again."

— Eleanor Ashworth

After completing a Masters degree in photographic conservation at the Rochester Institute of Technology, I returned to Scotland and began working systematically on private and institutional collections. The Silvered Glass Archive grew out of this practice — initially as a personal record of the work, and gradually as a public resource.

The archive now holds digitised records of over two hundred and forty photographs spanning the period 1839 to 1915. Each has been assessed, scanned, and where appropriate restored, with full documentation of every intervention.

Approach & Ethics

My approach to restoration is governed by a single principle: honesty. I never invent detail that was not there in the original. I work from evidence — tonal values, process characteristics, comparable examples — and I document every decision.

This means researching subjects where possible — in census records, trade directories, local newspapers, church registers. And it means being honest when we do not know: many of the people in these photographs will remain anonymous, and we should acknowledge that rather than fabricating a story.

Commissions & Enquiries

I accept a limited number of private restoration commissions each year. If you have Victorian or Edwardian photographs in need of assessment or restoration, please use the contact page.

A Note on Dignity

Many of the people in this archive are unknown to us. They sat before a camera at some point in their lives, perhaps on the occasion of a marriage or a departure, perhaps simply because they could.

They could not have imagined that their image would survive them by a century and a half. We try to honour that strangeness with something approaching the respect we would show to a living person.

✦   ✦   ✦

That means captioning carefully — not filling silence with invention. It means acknowledging what we do not know. And it means approaching every face with the recognition that this was a life as complex and irreducible as any of ours.
