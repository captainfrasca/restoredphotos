David van Coolwijk

Independent Historian, Storyteller & Digital Restoration Artist · The Netherlands

David van Coolwijk is a Dutch independent historian, storyteller, and digital restoration artist dedicated to preserving fragments of the past that would otherwise fade into silence. His work focuses on the careful restoration of historical photographs — particularly images from the Victorian and Edwardian eras — with the goal of returning dignity, clarity, and human presence to moments captured more than a century ago.

Rather than treating photographs as simple images, David approaches them as historical documents. Every crease, stain, and faded detail is studied before any restoration begins. His method emphasises preservation of identity above all else: faces are never altered, expressions remain untouched, and the original composition is respected. The aim is not to modernise the past, but to reveal it as faithfully as possible.

"Each photograph represents a real person who lived, worked, loved, and existed in a world that is now gone. By restoring these images, I hope to bridge the distance between past and present."

— David van Coolwijk

Through meticulous digital restoration, damaged photographs regain lost detail while retaining their authentic historical character. In some cases, historically accurate colourisation is applied to help modern audiences connect more deeply with the people in the images. When appropriate, David also explores modern reinterpretations of archival photographs — imagining how the same scene might appear if captured with contemporary photographic technology, while strictly preserving the identities and humanity of the subjects.

Philosophy

For David, restoration is more than a technical process — it is a form of historical care. Each photograph represents a real person who lived, worked, loved, and existed in a world that is now gone. Restoration is never about invention; it is about listening carefully to what remains and allowing it to speak again.

The Archive

His work is shared through personal archival projects and historical websites, where restored photographs become part of a living memory — not simply artefacts of history, but windows into the lives of those who came before us. On Forgotten Faces

Many of the people in this archive are unknown to us by name. But they were real — they sat before a camera at some point in their lives, held still, and allowed themselves to be seen. That act of stillness, however brief, was an act of trust.

They could not have imagined that their image would survive them by a century and a half, to be viewed by strangers on a screen in a world utterly transformed. We carry that knowledge with us in every restoration.

✦   ✦   ✦

Restoration is not about making the past look modern. It is about removing the noise of time so that what was always there — a face, an expression, a human presence — can be seen again. Not as history. As a person.
