The Archivist

About the Archive

The Silvered Glass Archive was founded in 2015 by photographic conservator Eleanor Ashworth, with the mission of recovering and contextualising the photographic heritage of the Victorian and Edwardian eras.

Eleanor Ashworth

Photographic Conservator & Historian · Edinburgh, Scotland

I came to photographic restoration by an indirect route. As a student of art history in Edinburgh, I spent much of my time in the archives of the Scottish National Portrait Gallery, where I first encountered the extraordinary richness of Victorian photography — and the heartbreaking fragility of those early images.

A chance afternoon with a collection of deteriorating albumen prints belonging to a retired schoolteacher in Perthshire convinced me that there was serious and important work to be done. The photographs were beautiful. They were also disintegrating. Nobody in the family knew who the subjects were. Nobody had looked at them in perhaps fifty years.

"These people sat in front of a camera at some point in their lives and were still. They wanted to be seen. We owe it to them to make that possible again."

— Eleanor Ashworth

After completing a Masters degree in photographic conservation at the Rochester Institute of Technology, I returned to Scotland and began working systematically on private and institutional collections. The Silvered Glass Archive grew out of this practice — initially as a personal record of the work, and gradually as a public resource.

The archive now holds digitised records of over two hundred and forty photographs spanning the period 1839 to 1915, representing fourteen countries and a remarkable range of photographic processes. Each has been assessed, scanned, and where appropriate restored, with full documentation of every intervention.

Approach & Ethics

My approach to restoration is governed by a single principle: honesty. I never invent detail that was not there in the original. I work from evidence — tonal values, process characteristics, comparable examples — and I document every decision. The unaltered scan is always archived alongside the restored image.

I am also committed to historical contextualisation. A photograph is not merely a visual object — it is a document, a social artefact, an intersection of technology, economics, and individual life. Understanding the context of each image is as important as understanding its chemistry.

This means researching subjects where possible — in census records, trade directories, local newspapers, church registers. It means understanding the studio that made the photograph, the process used, the conventions of the period. And it means being honest when we do not know: many of the people in these photographs will remain anonymous, and we should acknowledge that rather than fabricating a story.

Commissions & Enquiries

I accept a limited number of private restoration commissions each year, with a preference for photographs with family or local historical significance. I also work with institutions — county archives, local museums, historical societies — on larger collection projects.

If you have Victorian or Edwardian photographs in need of assessment or restoration, I would be glad to hear from you. Please use the contact page to describe your photographs and their condition.

A Brief History

The Archive's Story

2008

First Encounter

A collection of deteriorating albumen prints in Perthshire. The beginning of a serious engagement with Victorian photographic conservation.

2010–2012

Rochester Institute of Technology

Postgraduate study in photographic conservation, with specialisation in nineteenth-century processes and digital restoration methodology.

2013–2014

Early Practice

Private restoration commissions and work with the Scottish Borders Archive. First institutional partnership with a regional museum collection.

2015

Archive Founded

The Silvered Glass Archive formally established as a public digital resource. Initial collection of forty-seven photographs from eleven private donors.

2018

Royal Photographic Society Fellowship

Elected Fellow of the Royal Photographic Society in recognition of work in photographic conservation and historical research.

2020–Present

Continued Growth

The archive now holds records of over two hundred and forty photographs. An ongoing partnership with three county archives supports regular new acquisitions.

A Note on Dignity

Many of the people in this archive are unknown to us. They sat before a camera at some point in their lives, perhaps on the occasion of a marriage or a departure, perhaps to send an image to a distant relative, perhaps simply because they could — photography, by the 1860s, was within reach of most working people.

They could not have imagined that their image would survive them by a century and a half, to be viewed by strangers on a screen. We try to honour that strangeness with something approaching the respect we would show to a living person.

That means captioning carefully — not filling silence with invention. It means acknowledging what we do not know. And it means approaching every face with the recognition that this was a life as complex and irreducible as any of ours.
