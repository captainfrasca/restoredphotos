Conservation & Restoration

The Art of Recovery

Each photograph tells a story. Our restoration work aims to recover what time has obscured — with honesty, care, and reverence for the original.

How We Work

Our Restoration Methodology

A disciplined process rooted in photographic history and conservation ethics.

I

Acquisition & Assessment

Every photograph is assessed for condition, provenance, and photographic process. We document all damage before any work begins — foxing, fading, tears, silver mirroring, and chemical decay are mapped carefully.

II

High-Resolution Scanning

Originals are scanned at a minimum of 1200 dpi using a flatbed drum scanner with custom ICC profiles. Glass plates and daguerreotypes require specialised raking-light setups to reveal surface detail invisible to normal illumination.

III

Digital Restoration

Using 16-bit colour editing workflows, we address each area of damage individually. Tone reconstruction is guided by other extant examples of the same photographic process. No invented detail is introduced.

IV

Review & Documentation

Every intervention is documented and a full restoration record is kept. The unaltered scan is always archived alongside the restored version. We are transparent about what has been done to every image.

V

Physical Conservation

Where possible, we work with a partner conservator to stabilise the physical original — using archival housing, controlled humidity, and where appropriate, photographic retouching of the print itself using conservation-grade materials.

VI

Historical Contextualisation

Every photograph is researched for its historical context. We consult census records, trade directories, local archives, and specialist historians to place each image in its proper time and place.

Before After

Iago — Julia Margaret Cameron

Albumen Silver Print · c.1867

This dramatic portrait by Julia Margaret Cameron arrived in the archive with significant silver mirroring across the upper third, a crease running diagonally through the subject's shoulder, and widespread loss of tonal range in the shadows due to fading of the silver image.

The restoration addressed each of these issues in sequence. Silver mirroring — a form of corrosion visible as a metallic sheen — was carefully mapped and its tonal values reconstructed from the original shadow densities visible at the edges of the affected area. The crease was reduced using frequency separation techniques that preserve the underlying photographic information.

"Cameron's genius lay in her deliberate softness — the out-of-focus quality that her contemporaries criticised and posterity has come to revere."

— Restoration notes, 2021

The photograph now reads as Cameron would have intended: a face emerging from shadow with the quality of a Renaissance oil painting, the sitter's expression holding its full weight and ambiguity.

Before After

Brig upon the Water — Le Gray

Albumen Print from Waxed Paper Negative · c.1856

Gustave Le Gray solved one of the central problems of early photography — capturing both sea and sky in a single image — through his technique of combining two separate negatives. The version in our collection showed considerable yellowing of the albumen layer and a network of fine cracks throughout the sky portion.

The crack network in the sky was the most delicate aspect of the restoration. Rather than simply cloning adjacent areas, we used a guided synthesis approach based on the tonal values of the original waxed paper negative process, ensuring that the repaired areas retained the characteristic soft luminosity of Le Gray's skies.

Yellowing was addressed through careful colour profile reconstruction based on known examples of well-preserved Le Gray albumen prints held in major museum collections.

Before After

Power House Mechanic

Gelatin Silver Print · Lewis Hine · c.1920

Lewis Hine spent two decades documenting the human cost of industrialisation in America. This iconic image — a man in communion with a vast steam pump — arrived with a large tear in the lower left quadrant and significant surface abrasion across the highlight areas of the machine.

The torn area required the most extensive intervention. We cross-referenced three other known prints of this negative to reconstruct the missing information. The surface abrasion in the highlights was addressed by rebuilding gradients from the undamaged portions of the original silver layer.

Hine's characteristic tonal range — deep, warm blacks against industrial greys — was carefully preserved throughout. The image retains all of its original gravitas and social power.

Historical Context

Photographic Processes of the Archive's Era

Understanding the original process is essential to any successful restoration.

1839

Daguerreotype

The first commercially viable photographic process, introduced by Louis Daguerre. Images are formed directly on a polished silver plate. Daguerreotypes are uniquely vulnerable to abrasion and tarnish, and must be viewed at the correct angle to be seen at all.

1841

Calotype (Talbotype)

William Henry Fox Talbot's paper negative process allowed for multiple prints from a single negative — the fundamental model of photography until the digital age. Calotype prints have a characteristically warm, matte surface and fine paper texture.

1851

Wet Collodion / Ambrotype

Frederick Scott Archer's glass negative process dominated professional photography for three decades. The ambrotype variant creates a positive image by backing the underexposed glass negative with black material, producing a rich, detailed image.

1854

Albumen Print

Coated with egg white sensitised with silver nitrate, albumen paper became the standard printing medium of the Victorian age. Characteristic yellowing and fading are the most common degradation products encountered in our restoration work.

1871

Gelatin Dry Plate

Richard Maddox's gelatin emulsion on glass made photography practical for amateurs and documentary workers alike. Faster, more stable, and requiring no chemical preparation in the field — this was the process that opened photography to the world.

1888–1900

Gelatin Silver Print & Roll Film

George Eastman's Kodak camera and cellulose roll film democratised photography entirely. The gelatin silver print became the standard of the twentieth century. Most photographs in the Edwardian portion of our collection use this process.
